01
Profile Foundations

Username, photo, name field

The first 0.4 seconds. Before they read a word.
EXPAND

The name field contains a keyword, not just their name

Bold "Name" field on Instagram is the #1 ranking signal for in-platform search. "Sarah Chen | Fitness Coach" beats "Sarah Chen" every time. Empty keyword field = invisible to search.

When someone searches "fitness coach Perth," Instagram only shows accounts that have those words in the name field. Not the bio. Not the captions. The name field.
Critical

Profile photo is recognisable at 40px

Open the profile on a phone, then squint. If you can't tell what it is, the algorithm's notification thumbnail and the Reels comment avatar already lost the battle. Faces beat logos for personal brands. Logos need bold contrast and transparent backgrounds.

Most discovery happens at thumbnail size — comments, search results, reel overlays. A pretty photo that goes muddy at 40px is a wasted asset.
Critical

Username is simple, memorable, near-identical across platforms

If their handle on Instagram is different from TikTok and YouTube, they're forcing every fan to re-find them. One handle, one search, three platforms.

Standard

Photo is consistent across LinkedIn, IG, and the website

The "five tab test" — open them in tabs side-by-side. If they look like five different people, every cross-platform discovery doubles back to "wait, is this even the same person?"

Standard
02
The Bio

150 characters of pitch and filter

Most profiles waste this real estate. Yours shouldn't.
EXPAND

The bio answers: what you do, who it's for, one proof point

"I help [audience] achieve [result] through [method/timeframe]." If a stranger reads the bio and can't tell those three things, the bio is decoration.

The bio is the conversion line between "saw a reel" and "pressed follow." It's a pitch, not a personality bio.
Critical

One primary CTA, not five

"Fewer, bigger, better." If the bio has a free guide AND a podcast AND a course AND a Discord AND a podcast clip, none of them get clicked. Pick the one that matches the goal, kill the rest from the bio (they can live in Linktree).

Every additional option cuts the click-through on the primary action. The bio is a funnel, not a menu.
Critical

Specific, not generic

"Helping you find your next favourite read — fantasy and romance only" beats "book lover and reader." Specificity is what makes the right person stop and the wrong person scroll. You want both.

Standard

Contact path is visible without opening the action button

Service businesses get inbound from desktop. Creators get inbound from brand scouts using filtering tools. Both miss the mobile-only "Email" button. Putting the email or booking URL in the actual bio text catches both.

Standard
03
Link, Highlights, Pinned

The conversion architecture

Where the follower becomes a lead, or doesn't.
EXPAND

Link-in-bio loads in under 2 seconds on 4G

If their Linktree is 12 buttons long, takes 4 seconds to load, and the lead magnet is button #7, nobody is getting to the lead magnet. Test it on cellular, not wifi.

Mobile users abandon at 3 seconds. Every extra second past 2 cuts conversion by ~20%.
Critical

The link points to one high-value destination

Not the homepage. A specific lead magnet, booking page, or landing page that matches the bio's promise. "Get my free 12-month creator plan" → a Stan store opt-in. Not "visit my website."

Bio promise → link destination = same offer. Mismatch kills the trust handoff.
Critical

Highlights: 5–7 categories, custom branded covers, themed

Service businesses: About / Services / Reviews / Case Studies / Free Guide / Contact. Personal brands: About / Free Guide / Wins / Behind the Scenes / Reviews / Work With Me. Default IG cover screenshots are a tell that nobody's actually thought about this.

Standard

Up to 3 pinned posts that reinforce the bio promise

Best testimonial, biggest viral tip, current offer. The top 3 grid slots are the most-viewed real estate on the entire profile. Pinning a random selfie there is wasting it.

A new visitor sees the bio, then their eyes drop to the pinned posts. That's two pieces of evidence in three seconds — make them line up.
Critical
04
Cover & Thumbnail

The grid-tap decision

Whether the video gets opened in the first place.
EXPAND

The cover reads cleanly at grid size

Reels are designed at 1080×1920 but the profile grid crops to 1:1 (or 3:4 in some rollouts). If the headline text is at the top of the frame, the grid crops it off. Test by viewing the profile, not just the reel.

The cover is doing the same job as a YouTube thumbnail — making someone tap. A cropped cover is a missed tap.
Critical

Cover text is short, high-contrast, centred

Three to six words max. White text with black stroke, or the inverse. Centred, not pushed to an edge. The eye should land on it before the brain processes the image.

Standard

Cover and verbal hook do not say the same thing

Same trap as YouTube — title and thumbnail shouldn't duplicate. Cover text should summarise the gift; the verbal hook should create curiosity. Saying the same thing twice wastes one of them.

Standard

Grid as a whole has visual coherence

Step back from the profile. Does it look like one person's body of work, or 30 random posts? A consistent colour, a consistent text treatment, even just consistent framing — any one of those signals "this person is intentional."

Standard
05
The First 3 Seconds

The hook layering test

Up to 50% of viewers leave here. Get this right and reach 5–10x.
EXPAND

Visual hook: first frame stops the scroll

High contrast, a face with direct eye contact, unexpected motion, or a result before the explanation. Not a slow pan. Not a logo intro. Not "hey guys welcome back."

The first frame is the static thumbnail in someone's feed for the half-second before autoplay catches up. It has to do work even with no audio and no motion.
Critical

Text hook visible in the first 1.5 seconds — different from the audio

Most reels are watched on mute. The text needs to land before IG's caption overlay loads. Ten words or fewer. And it should not duplicate what's being said — text summarises the gift, audio creates curiosity.

Test it: mute the video, watch the first 1.5 seconds, ask "would I keep watching?" If no, the hook fails.
Critical

Audio hook: cold-open, contrarian take, or pain point — not throat-clearing

"Most personal trainers will lie to you about this." "I lost $40k learning this." "Your bio is the reason nobody's converting." Versus: "Hey everyone! So today I wanted to share..." The first three rip into curiosity. The last one announces a video is starting.

Critical

Three layers, not one

The best hooks layer all three — visual + text + audio — in the first 3 seconds. One layer alone isn't enough anymore. Watch the first 3 seconds of their last 5 reels. Count the layers. Most fail this test.

Standard
06
Retention & Pacing

What happens after the hook

Watch time is the #1 algo signal in 2026. This is where it's earned or lost.
EXPAND

Visual cut every 1.5–3 seconds

Static shot held longer than 4 seconds = drop-off zone. Doesn't have to be a hard edit — a zoom, a cutaway, a text change all count as a visual reset.

The brain treats unchanging visual input as "nothing's happening." It scrolls. Visual change is what tells the brain "still worth watching."
Critical

Length earns its place — most reels should be 7–25 seconds

7–15s for one-liner reach. 15–30s for instructional. 30–60s only if every second earns it. Going longer to "add value" usually adds water. Shorter beats longer unless retention data proves otherwise.

Critical

Loop-close ending or sends-on rewatch

Best-performing reels end in a way that punches the viewer back to the beginning, or makes them think "wait, what?" and rewatch. Ending with "follow for more" is leaving retention on the table.

Standard

No filler in seconds 8–25

That's the high-density value zone. If they're saying "and another thing I want to mention is," that's filler. Cut it.

Standard
07
Captions & On-Screen Text

The mute test

Most viewers hear nothing. Make sure they still get the message.
EXPAND

Burned-in subtitles, not auto-captions

Auto-captions break, miss words, vanish off-screen. Burned-in (CapCut, Premiere) lets you control the timing, font, colour, and stroke. For branded content this is non-negotiable.

Auto-captions are a tell that the video was uploaded as an afterthought. Burned-in is a tell that someone thought about how it'd be consumed.
Critical

Bold sans-serif, ≥45px, white with 2px black stroke

Anything thinner is unreadable on a busy background. Anything smaller breaks on small phones. The 2px stroke is what stops it disappearing into a light frame.

Standard

Primary keyword in the on-screen text within the first 3 seconds

Instagram OCRs on-screen text and uses it for content classification. The word that defines what this video is about should appear early and visibly.

Standard

Caption first line is the second hook

The visible portion before "...more" is doing real work. Don't waste it on "watch till the end" — use it to extend curiosity or pre-frame the takeaway.

Standard
08
Safe Zones & Format

Where the platform crops the work

Critical text and faces in the wrong zone = invisible.
EXPAND

All critical content lives in the central 70–80% of the frame

Between roughly y=285px and y=1635px on the 1080×1920 canvas. The top has the camera/status bar; the bottom has the IG caption, audio info, and engagement buttons.

Text below y=1640px gets buried under IG's UI. Text above y=250px gets cropped on grid view. Both zones are dead.
Critical

Bottom 280px is clear of headlines or faces

That's where IG stacks the username, caption, audio strip, and the like/comment/share buttons. If the verbal hook is in that zone, half of it is hidden behind UI on a real phone.

Critical

Right edge has 90px buffer

The like, comment, share, save, and "more" buttons live there. Faces or text crammed into the right margin get partially covered.

Standard

Aspect ratio is 9:16, 1080×1920, 30fps minimum

Anything else gets letterboxed or downscaled. Square posts in a Reel grid kill reach.

Standard
09
Algorithm Hygiene

The unforced errors

Things that quietly suppress reach without anyone realising.
EXPAND

No watermarks from TikTok, CapCut, or other platforms

Instagram's 2026 Originality Score actively penalises content with foreign watermarks. If the reel has a TikTok logo on it, reach is being suppressed before the algorithm even decides whether the content is good.

This is the easiest fix and the one most reposters miss. Re-export from CapCut without the watermark, or re-cut natively.
Critical

3–5 specific niche hashtags, not 30

Hashtags are filing labels in 2026, not growth levers. Hashtag-heavy posts now perform ~23% worse than minimal-hashtag posts. Three to five tight, niche tags is the new ceiling.

Critical

Original audio or trending audio — not muted

A reel without audio is a graphic post in a video format. The algorithm treats audio (especially original or trending) as a context signal. Silent reels lose that signal.

Standard

Posting cadence they can sustain for 12 months

Not "what's optimal." What's sustainable. Three reels a week for 52 weeks beats daily for 8 weeks then ghosting. The cadence question is "what's your minimum?" not "what's your max?"

Standard

Account is positioned for one niche, not five

Instagram assigns one topic per account in 2026 — they call it "account-based embedding." If the last 9 reels are fitness, business, mum life, and fashion, the algo can't decide who to show it to. Pick the topic, hold the line for 90 days, watch what happens.
